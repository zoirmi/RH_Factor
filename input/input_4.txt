      Artist Biography by David Jeffries

A Livingroom Hush
<https://www.allmusic.com/album/a-livingroom-hush-mw0000660007>
Boundary-crashing Norwegian big band Jaga Jazzist
<https://www.allmusic.com/artist/jaga-jazzist-mn0000128331> have found
favor among fans of post-rock, progressive jazz, and left-field dance
music, among other styles. Led by composer and multi-instrumentalist
Lars Horntveth
<https://www.allmusic.com/artist/lars-horntveth-mn0000067124>, the group
sports an ever-shifting lineup comprised of some of the most talented
musicians from Norway's jazz and experimental music scenes. On albums
such as 2001's A Livingroom Hush
<https://www.allmusic.com/album/a-livingroom-hush-mw0000660007>, the
group spiked their rhapsodic, energetic jazz tunes with glitches, noise
effects, and breakbeat-like drumming, splicing influences such as
Tortoise <https://www.allmusic.com/artist/tortoise-mn0000180023>,
Charles Mingus
<https://www.allmusic.com/artist/charles-mingus-mn0000009680>, and
Squarepusher
<https://www.allmusic.com/artist/squarepusher-mn0000158849>. Later
efforts such as 2010's One-Armed Bandit
<https://www.allmusic.com/album/one-armed-bandit-mw0001954859> were less
electronic and closer to progressive rock, with 2015's Starfire
<https://www.allmusic.com/album/starfire-mw0002838708> and 2020's
Pyramid <https://www.allmusic.com/album/pyramid-mw0003362784> consisting
of lengthy suites, as opposed to the shorter songs of their earlier albums.

Magazine <https://www.allmusic.com/album/magazine-mw0000633596> The
sprawling jazz-meets-electronics ensemble came to life in 1994 when
their main brain and songwriter, Lars Horntveth
<https://www.allmusic.com/artist/lars-horntveth-mn0000067124>, was only
14. Two years later their debut album, Jævla Jazzist Grete Stitz,
appeared, and Norwegians got their first taste of the band's combination
of jazz chops and electronic quirkiness, which additionally incorporated
genres such as rap and reggae. Two years after that the Magazine
<https://www.allmusic.com/album/magazine-mw0000633596> EP appeared, but
it was 2001's A Livingroom Hush
<https://www.allmusic.com/album/a-livingroom-hush-mw0000660007> that
grabbed all the attention due to rave reviews and distribution by Warner
Bros. in their homeland. Coldcut
<https://www.allmusic.com/artist/coldcut-mn0000092226>'s label, Ninja
Tune, picked the album up for worldwide distribution in 2002 and BBC
radio listeners crowned it the Jazz Album of the Year.

The Animal Chin
<https://www.allmusic.com/album/animal-chin-mw0000663024> EP appeared in
early 2003, introducing Jaga Jazzist to American listeners, and was
followed by The Stix
<https://www.allmusic.com/album/the-stix-mw0000035266>, an album that
found the band using more electronics and drum'n'bass-inspired rhythms.
The more rock-influenced What We Must
<https://www.allmusic.com/album/what-we-must-mw0000255755> followed in
2005 before a five-year break took the band out of the studio and off
the road. Jaga Jazzist
<https://www.allmusic.com/artist/jaga-jazzist-mn0000128331> returned in
2010 with One-Armed Bandit
<https://www.allmusic.com/album/one-armed-bandit-mw0001954859>,
featuring new recruit Stian Westerhus
<https://www.allmusic.com/artist/stian-westerhus-mn0002124287> on
guitar, though he left the group after that recording.

Live with Britten Sinfonia
<https://www.allmusic.com/album/live-with-britten-sinfonia-mw0002496487>
The band's next project was its most ambitious to date: a concert
recording with the 25-member Britten Sinfonia
<https://www.allmusic.com/artist/britten-sinfonia-mn0001632092>, which
found the group in a sprawling, colorful collaboration with one of
England's finest modern classical orchestras to present something that
stretched the creative boundaries of both groups. The recorded evidence,
Live with the Britten Sinfonia
<https://www.allmusic.com/album/live-with-the-britten-sinfonia-mw0002496487>,
was issued in May of 2013. A suite of songs based on the constellations,
and how views of the stars change around the globe, the 2015 album
Starfire <https://www.allmusic.com/album/starfire-mw0002838708> was
influenced by Horntveth
<https://www.allmusic.com/artist/horntveth-mn0000067124>'s move from
Norway to Los Angeles. "Prokrastinopel," a non-album single featuring
Swedish guitarist Reine Fiske
<https://www.allmusic.com/artist/reine-fiske-mn0000940892> (Dungen
<https://www.allmusic.com/artist/dungen-mn0000611051>, Fire! Orchestra
<https://www.allmusic.com/artist/fire%21-orchestra-mn0003038715>),
appeared in 2017. The group returned in 2020 with Pyramid
<https://www.allmusic.com/album/pyramid-mw0003362784>, a set of four
lengthy compositions recorded in just two weeks, making the release one
of their most spontaneous efforts. The self-produced album appeared on
Brainfeeder, the Flying Lotus
<https://www.allmusic.com/artist/flying-lotus-mn0000717419>-curated Los
Angeles imprint.
